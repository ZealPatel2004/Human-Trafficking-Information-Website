Executive Summary & Background

Date: August 12, 2025

Human trafficking—defined by the United Nations as the recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons through force, fraud, or coercion for the purpose of exploitation—remains one of the world’s most pervasive human rights abuses. It exploits adults and children in forced labor, commercial sexual exploitation, forced criminality, and other forms of coercion. Recent research shows that organized criminal networks have adapted their tactics, increasingly using the internet and deceptive job offers to recruit victims, while conflicts, economic shocks, and weak rule of law continue to increase vulnerability.

This report synthesizes current evidence, major trends since 2021, and policy responses across prevention, protection, and prosecution, and offers actionable recommendations.

Scope and Definitions

This report uses the framework of the UN Palermo Protocol and the U.S. Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA). “Human trafficking” involves: (1) an act (recruitment, transport, transfer, harboring, receipt of persons); (2) means (threat or use of force, coercion, abduction, fraud, deception, abuse of power, or giving/receiving payments to control a person); and (3) purpose (exploitation). For children, proof of “means” is not required. Exploitation includes forced labor, sexual exploitation, forced marriage, forced criminality, and organ removal (rare but documented).

Global Trends & Forms of Trafficking

Global Prevalence and Trends: Best global estimates of “modern slavery”—a broader umbrella that includes forced labor and forced marriage—indicate that about 50 million people were in situations of modern slavery in 2021, including roughly 28 million in forced labor and 22 million in forced marriage. Detection and convictions for trafficking fluctuate by region; while some countries have strengthened laws and victim identification, others have seen declines in detected cases since the pandemic due to diverted law enforcement resources, closed borders, and victims being pushed further underground.

Major Forms and Sectors

  • Forced labor in private economies (manufacturing, construction, agriculture, domestic work, fishing): Typically involves debt bondage, document confiscation, and threats of deportation.
  • Commercial sexual exploitation: Traffickers use fraud, grooming, and violence; online platforms are common for recruitment and advertising.
  • Forced criminality and cyber-enabled fraud: Since 2022, thousands have been trafficked into “fraud factory” compounds—initially in Southeast Asia and increasingly spreading to other regions—where victims are forced to run online scams (“pig-butchering,” investment fraud, romance scams) under threat of violence.
  • State-imposed forced labor: Reported in several contexts, including compulsory prison labor and abusive conscription.
  • Child trafficking: Children are trafficked for sexual exploitation, forced labor, forced marriage, and illegal adoption; they are especially vulnerable in humanitarian crises.

Drivers & Modus Operandi

Drivers and Risk Factors

Key risk factors include poverty and unemployment, irregular migration channels, discrimination (including against minorities and migrants), weak labor inspection, gender-based violence, corruption, and demand for cheap labor and commercial sex. Sudden shocks—conflict, disasters, public health emergencies—raise vulnerability by disrupting livelihoods and services. The increasing role of social media and messaging apps enables micro-targeted recruitment through deceptive job ads and romantic grooming. Meanwhile, crypto and online payment rails facilitate cross-border money movement for criminal networks.

Modus Operandi and Technology

Traffickers leverage mainstream platforms (social networks, job boards, encrypted messaging) to recruit and control victims. “Interpreter” technologies (AI translation, large language models) reduce the need for multilingual recruiters, while deepfake media can enhance deception. In scam compounds, traffickers confiscate passports, confine victims, and compel them to execute scripted social engineering campaigns at industrial scale. Emerging countermeasures include platform ad verification, takedown collaboration, and machine-learning tools to detect trafficking-linked content and patterns in hotlines or ads.

Legal & Policy Frameworks

Internationally, the Palermo Protocol (2000) underpins national anti-trafficking laws. The U.S. TVPA established the annual Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report, which ranks countries’ efforts (Tier 1–3) and spurs reforms. Many regions have specialized laws, action plans, and survivor services. Still, enforcement gaps persist: limited victim-centered investigations, low conviction rates in some jurisdictions, and weak labor protections. A critical norm is the “non‑punishment principle,” which holds that victims should not be prosecuted for crimes they were compelled to commit (e.g., immigration violations, fraud executed under coercion).

Notable Developments (2024–2025)

  • Scam compounds and forced criminality: UN and research organizations document a global spread of forced scamming operations beyond Southeast Asia to parts of Africa, the Middle East, and the Americas. Victims are often lured by tech or customer‑service job ads, then confined and forced to defraud others online.
  • Regional enforcement drives and cooperation: Cross‑border task forces have rescued victims and disrupted compounds, but operators adapt by relocating and fragmenting operations.
  • Evolving online recruitment: Platforms report increased takedowns of deceptive job listings and trafficking‑linked accounts; however, detection remains challenging due to localized slang, ephemeral accounts, and encrypted channels.
  • Legislative activity: Countries continue to update anti‑trafficking statutes, national referral mechanisms, and financial crime tools to disrupt profits and provide services to survivors.

Challenges in Measurement & Data

Trafficking is hidden by design. Many victims never self‑identify or reach authorities due to fear, stigma, or immigration concerns. Administrative data reflect law‑enforcement activity more than true prevalence. Household and business surveys face ethical and methodological limits. The result is wide uncertainty intervals in global estimates and lags (e.g., 2021 baselines). Nonetheless, triangulating multiple data sources—law enforcement, hotlines, NGO service records, migration data, and financial intelligence—can illuminate patterns and priority areas for interventions.

Case Illustrations

  • Deceptive tech jobs to forced scamming: Young, educated job seekers respond to online ads for “IT support” or “digital marketing” roles in Thailand or Cambodia. Upon arrival, passports are seized; victims are forced to run romance-investment scams targeting foreigners. Those who resist face beatings, electric shocks, and starvation. Some victims are re‑trafficked as recruiters.
  • Domestic work coercion: Migrant domestic workers pay illegal recruitment fees, incur debt, and have their passports withheld. Employers impose 18-hour days, restrict movement, and threaten deportation. Many jurisdictions lack effective complaint mechanisms or allow tied visas that heighten dependency.

How AI Can Help

1. Detection of Trafficking Activity

  • Online Ad & Social Media Monitoring: AI models can scan millions of job postings, dating profiles, or escort ads to detect suspicious patterns—e.g., repeated phrases, coded language, unusual photo metadata.
  • Image & Video Recognition: Facial recognition (with strong privacy safeguards) can help identify missing persons in online content, CCTV footage, or border cameras.
  • Natural Language Processing (NLP): AI can flag recruitment messages or conversations showing signs of grooming, coercion, or deceptive job offers.

2. Disrupting Criminal Networks

  • Financial Intelligence: AI can analyze bank transactions, cryptocurrency flows, and remittance patterns to detect trafficking-related money laundering.
  • Network Analysis: Machine learning can map links between suspects, shell companies, online accounts, and known trafficking hubs.
  • Dark Web Monitoring: AI tools can crawl hidden marketplaces for trafficking-related ads and coordinate takedowns with law enforcement.

3. Victim Identification & Protection

  • Hotline & Tip Analysis: AI can triage reports from hotlines, emails, and texts to prioritize urgent cases and route them to the right agencies.
  • Language Translation: Real-time AI translation can help victims communicate across language barriers with police or aid workers.
  • Pattern Recognition in Service Data: AI can detect recurring victim profiles (e.g., similar recruitment methods) to help prevention agencies target outreach.

4. Prevention & Public Awareness

  • Targeted Awareness Campaigns: AI-powered ad targeting can reach at-risk communities with digital safety tips and alerts about scam job postings.
  • Job Ad Verification: Platforms can use AI to verify employer legitimacy before publishing high-risk job ads, reducing recruitment opportunities.
  • School & Workplace Education: AI chatbots can deliver safe, anonymous education on trafficking warning signs.
